# Spring Planting Season 2026: Your Guide to Growing in the UK

Spring is an exciting time for gardeners across the United Kingdom. As the days lengthen and temperatures begin to rise, the soil awakens from its winter dormancy, creating the perfect conditions for sowing seeds and planting out young transplants. Whether you're a seasoned allotment holder or a first-time gardener, spring 2026 offers a fresh opportunity to cultivate your plot and enjoy the fruits of your labour.

## Preparing Your Garden

Before you begin planting, proper preparation is essential for a successful growing season. Start by clearing any remaining winter debris from your beds and borders. Remove weeds while they're still small and easy to manage, as they'll compete with your crops for nutrients, water, and sunlight.

Testing your soil is a crucial step that many gardeners overlook. The UK has a diverse range of soil types, from the heavy clay of the Midlands to the sandy soils of East Anglia. A simple pH test will help you understand what amendments might be needed. Most vegetables thrive in slightly acidic to neutral soil with a pH between 6.0 and 7.0. Adding well-rotted compost or manure will improve soil structure and fertility, ensuring your plants have the best possible start.

Allow the soil to warm up before heavy cultivation. Working wet soil can damage its structure, leading to compaction problems later in the season. In most regions of England, Scotland, and Wales, soil temperatures typically reach plantable levels by mid to late April, though this varies depending on your local climate and microclimate.

## What to Grow

The possibilities for spring planting are extensive, particularly in the UK's temperate climate. Hardy vegetables such as broad beans, peas, and early brassicas can be sown directly outdoors from March onwards. These crops tolerate the variable spring weather and will establish strong root systems before the growing season really takes off.

Root vegetables like carrots, beetroot, and turnips can also be sown in spring, provided the soil is workable and not waterlogged. Successional sowing every few weeks will ensure a continuous harvest throughout summer and autumn.

Courgettes, squash, and beans are best started indoors in modules or small pots in March or early April, then transplanted outside once all risk of frost has passed—typically after the May bank holidays in most southern regions, and as late as early June in more northerly areas.

Don't forget about herbs! Basil, coriander, and parsley can be grown on a sunny windowsill now for transplanting later, while hardy herbs like rosemary, thyme, and chives can be divided and planted out directly.

## Essential Tips for Success

Monitor local weather conditions carefully. The UK is famous for its unpredictable spring weather, with late frosts capable of damaging tender seedlings. Keep horticultural fleece or cloches handy for covering vulnerable plants during cold snaps. investing in a min/max thermometer will help you track soil and air temperatures accurately.

Water consistently but mindfully. Spring rains can be deceptive—frequent light showers often don't penetrate deeply enough to reach plant roots. Check soil moisture regularly and water deeply when needed, preferably in the morning to reduce disease risk.

Finally, keep records of what you plant and when. Garden journals or even simple notes in your phone can prove invaluable for planning future seasons and learning from both successes and disappointments. Happy gardening!
